## PHASE 1: INTERNAL INTELLIGENCE (HOMEOSTASIS)

**Focus:** How the body maintains stability

### Lesson 1: The Balancing Act

**Focus:** Homeostasis introduction & Stimulus-Response Model

**Gamified Activity:** "Thermostat Control" - Students manually control a heater/cooler to keep a virtual room at 22¬∞C while random weather events happen outside

**Key Concept:** Negative Feedback Loops

**Duration:** 20-25 minutes

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Define homeostasis
- Identify stimulus and response in body systems
- Explain negative feedback loops
- Apply homeostasis to temperature regulation

**Misconceptions to Address:**
- Homeostasis means "staying exactly the same" (it's dynamic balance within a range)
- Body temperature is always exactly 37¬∞C (it varies slightly)
- Only one system controls each variable (multiple systems coordinate)

---

### Lesson 2: The Surveillance Network

**Focus:** Detecting changes (Receptors & Sense Organs)

**Interactive:** "Wire the Body" - Drag and drop nerves from receptors (eyes, skin, ears) to the brain

**Lab Link:** Reaction Time practical (virtual or physical)

**Duration:** 20-25 minutes

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Identify types of receptors (photoreceptors, thermoreceptors, mechanoreceptors, chemoreceptors, pain receptors)
- Explain how receptors detect stimuli
- Describe nerve pathways from receptors to brain
- Measure reaction time as indicator of nervous system speed

**Misconceptions to Address:**
- Pain is always bad (it's a protective warning signal)
- We have only 5 senses (we have many more: balance, proprioception, temperature, etc.)
- Nerves send messages slowly (nerve signals travel up to 120 m/s!)

---

### Lesson 3: Central Command

**Focus:** The Nervous System (CNS vs PNS) & The Brain

**3D Model:** "Brain Builder" - Students assemble the lobes of the brain and label their functions (e.g., Occipital = Vision)

**Activity:** "Neuron Run" - A mini-game guiding an electrical impulse down an axon

**Duration:** 20-25 minutes

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Distinguish CNS (brain + spinal cord) from PNS (nerves throughout body)
- Identify brain lobes and their functions (frontal, parietal, temporal, occipital)
- Describe neuron structure (dendrites, cell body, axon, synapse)
- Explain how electrical signals travel along neurons

**Misconceptions to Address:**
- We only use 10% of our brain (we use all of it, just not all at once)
- Brain damage is always permanent (neuroplasticity allows some recovery)
- Nerves regenerate quickly like skin (nerve regeneration is very slow)

---

### Lesson 4: Chemical Messengers

**Focus:** The Endocrine System

**Analogy:** Nerves are text messages (fast/short); Hormones are letters in the mail (slow/long-lasting)

**Duration:** 20-25 minutes

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Compare nervous system (fast, short-term, electrical) vs endocrine system (slow, long-term, chemical)
- Identify major endocrine glands (pituitary, thyroid, pancreas, adrenals, ovaries/testes)
- Explain how hormones travel via bloodstream to target organs
- Describe hormone functions (growth, metabolism, blood sugar, stress response, reproduction)

**Misconceptions to Address:**
- Hormones only affect teenagers (hormones regulate body functions at all ages)
- Nervous and endocrine systems work separately (they coordinate closely)
- Hormones cause instant effects like nerves (hormones act slowly over minutes to hours)

---

### Lesson 5: System Failure (Diabetes)

**Focus:** Feedback loops & Blood Sugar regulation

**Error Analysis:** "Fix the Loop" - Students are shown a broken insulin feedback loop and must identify the missing component (Pancreas? Insulin receptor?)

**Duration:** 20-25 minutes

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Explain blood glucose regulation via insulin and glucagon (negative feedback)
- Compare Type 1 diabetes (pancreas produces no insulin) vs Type 2 (cells resist insulin)
- Identify consequences of uncontrolled blood sugar (hyperglycemia, hypoglycemia)
- Describe how feedback loops maintain homeostasis

**Misconceptions to Address:**
- Diabetes is caused by eating too much sugar (Type 1 is autoimmune, Type 2 has multiple causes including genetics)
- Only overweight people get diabetes (Type 1 is not weight-related)
- Insulin is a cure (it's management, not cure)

## PHASE 2: EXTERNAL THREATS (INFECTIOUS DISEASE)

**Focus:** Pathogens and transmission

### Lesson 6: The Rogue's Gallery

**Focus:** Infectious vs Non-infectious & Types of Pathogens

**Card Sort:** "Friend or Foe?" Sort bacteria, viruses, fungi, and protozoa

**Visual:** Comparing the size/structure of a Virus vs. Bacteria

**Duration:** 20-25 minutes

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Distinguish infectious (caused by pathogens, can spread) vs non-infectious disease (genetic, lifestyle, environmental - cannot spread)
- Compare four pathogen types: bacteria, viruses, fungi, protozoa
- Identify size differences (viruses smallest, bacteria small, fungi/protozoa larger)
- Understand that not all bacteria/fungi are harmful (many beneficial!)

**Misconceptions to Address:**
- All bacteria cause disease (most are harmless or beneficial - gut bacteria, nitrogen fixation)
- Viruses are alive (they're not - need host cells to reproduce)
- Antibiotics kill all germs (only bacteria, not viruses/fungi/protozoa)

---

### Lesson 7: Chain of Reaction

**Focus:** How diseases spread (Direct, Indirect, Vector)

**Simulation:** "The Sneeze" - Visualize droplet spread with/without masks and ventilation

**Duration:** 20-25 minutes

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Identify transmission methods:
  - Direct contact (touching, kissing, sexual contact)
  - Indirect contact (contaminated surfaces, food, water)
  - Droplet (coughing, sneezing)
  - Airborne (particles remain in air)
  - Vector (mosquitoes, ticks, fleas carry pathogen)
- Explain how barriers prevent transmission (masks, handwashing, safe food handling, vector control)
- Apply knowledge to real diseases (COVID-19, influenza, malaria, cholera)

**Misconceptions to Address:**
- All diseases spread the same way (transmission varies by pathogen)
- Masks only protect others, not you (quality masks protect both)
- Cold weather causes colds (viruses cause colds; we're just indoors more in winter)

---

### Lesson 8: Outbreak Simulator

**Focus:** Epidemics vs Pandemics

**Game:** "Patient Zero" - Watch a node network turn red as infection spreads. Students apply interventions (Hand washing, Quarantine) to flatten the curve

**Duration:** 20-25 minutes

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Define epidemic (widespread in one region) vs pandemic (worldwide)
- Explain R‚ÇÄ (basic reproduction number - how many people one infected person infects)
- Identify interventions: quarantine, isolation, social distancing, hygiene, vaccination
- Understand "flattening the curve" (spreading infections over time to avoid overwhelming healthcare)

**Misconceptions to Address:**
- Epidemic and pandemic mean the same thing (pandemic is global, epidemic is regional)
- Only deadly diseases cause pandemics (severity and spread are different - COVID spread widely, had moderate mortality)
- Herd immunity means everyone is immune (just enough people to protect vulnerable)

## PHASE 3: THE DEFENSE SYSTEMS (IMMUNE RESPONSE)

**Focus:** Lines of defense and immunity

### Lesson 9: The Castle Walls (1st & 2nd Line)

**Focus:** Skin, Mucous, Inflammation, Phagocytosis

**Mini-Game:** "Phagocyte Pac-Man" - Play as a Macrophage eating green bacteria blobs

**Duration:** 20-25 minutes

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Describe 1st line of defense (barriers):
  - Skin (physical barrier, slightly acidic, sebum)
  - Mucous membranes (trap pathogens)
  - Stomach acid (kills ingested pathogens)
  - Tears/saliva (contain lysozyme enzyme)
- Describe 2nd line of defense (non-specific cellular):
  - Inflammation (redness, swelling, heat, pain - increases blood flow, brings white blood cells)
  - Phagocytosis (macrophages and neutrophils engulf pathogens)
  - Fever (raises temperature - inhibits pathogen growth, speeds immune response)

**Misconceptions to Address:**
- Fever is harmful and must always be reduced (mild fever helps fight infection)
- Inflammation is bad (it's a protective response, though chronic inflammation is problematic)
- Skin is just a covering (it's an active immune barrier with antimicrobial properties)

---

### Lesson 10: Special Forces (3rd Line)

**Focus:** Antibodies, B-Cells, T-Cells (Specific Immunity)

**Puzzle:** "Lock & Key" - Match the specific antibody shape to the viral antigen

**Duration:** 20-25 minutes

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Explain 3rd line of defense (specific/adaptive immunity):
  - Antigens (foreign proteins on pathogen surface)
  - B-cells produce antibodies (Y-shaped proteins that bind specific antigens)
  - T-cells (helper T-cells coordinate response, killer T-cells destroy infected cells)
  - Memory cells (remember pathogen for faster response next time)
- Describe lock-and-key specificity (one antibody fits one antigen)
- Explain primary vs secondary immune response (first exposure slow, subsequent exposures fast)

**Misconceptions to Address:**
- Antibodies kill pathogens directly (they mark them for destruction or neutralize them)
- T-cells and B-cells do the same thing (T-cells coordinate and kill infected cells, B-cells make antibodies)
- Immunity is immediate (takes days for first response, faster for subsequent exposures)

---

### Lesson 11: The Training Ground (Vaccination)

**Focus:** How vaccines work & Herd Immunity

**Simulation:** Adjust vaccination rates (50%, 75%, 95%) and release a virus to see if the vulnerable population survives

**Duration:** 20-25 minutes

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Explain how vaccines work (introduce harmless antigen ‚Üí immune system creates memory cells ‚Üí rapid response to real pathogen)
- Identify vaccine types (live attenuated, inactivated, subunit, mRNA)
- Describe herd immunity (when enough people are immune, disease can't spread - protects vulnerable)
- Calculate herd immunity threshold (varies by disease - measles ~95%, COVID ~70-85%)

**Misconceptions to Address:**
- Vaccines give you the disease (most don't contain live pathogens; side effects ‚â† disease)
- Natural immunity is always better (some diseases cause severe complications - vaccination safer)
- Herd immunity means 100% must be vaccinated (percentage varies by disease contagiousness)

## PHASE 1: THE BLUEPRINTS (ATOMIC THEORY)

**Focus:** The structure of matter

### Lesson 1: The Atom Builder

**Focus:** Protons, Neutrons, Electrons & Electron Shells

**Gamified Activity:** "Stable or Radioactive?" - Students drag subatomic particles into a nucleus. If the ratio is wrong, it explodes. If it's right, they unlock the element.

**Duration:** 20-25 minutes

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Identify the three subatomic particles: protons (+), neutrons (neutral), electrons (-)
- Explain atomic structure: nucleus (protons + neutrons) with electrons in shells
- Understand atomic number (number of protons) defines the element
- Describe electron shell arrangement (2, 8, 8 pattern for first 20 elements)
- Recognize stable vs unstable isotopes (neutron:proton ratio)

**Misconceptions to Address:**
- Electrons orbit like planets (they exist in probability clouds/orbitals, not fixed orbits)
- Atoms are mostly solid matter (atoms are mostly empty space - nucleus is tiny compared to electron cloud)
- All atoms of an element are identical (isotopes have different neutron numbers)

**Visual Elements:**
- 3D atom builder interface
- Particle drag-and-drop mechanics
- Stability indicator (glows green for stable, red flash and explosion for unstable)
- Element unlock achievement system

---

### Lesson 2: The Periodic Table

**Focus:** Metals, Non-metals, and Trends

**Mini-Game:** "Element Tetris" - Sort falling elements into their correct groups (Alkali metals, Noble gases) before the screen fills up

**Duration:** 20-25 minutes

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Navigate the periodic table structure (periods = rows, groups = columns)
- Distinguish metals (left/center - shiny, conductive, malleable) from non-metals (right - dull, insulators, brittle)
- Identify key groups: Alkali metals (Group 1), Halogens (Group 17), Noble gases (Group 18)
- Recognize periodic trends: reactivity, atomic size, metallic character
- Understand Mendeleev's organization by properties and atomic mass

**Misconceptions to Address:**
- Elements are arranged randomly (systematic organization by atomic number and properties)
- All metals are the same (metals have varying reactivity, melting points, density)
- Noble gases never react (they rarely react, but can under extreme conditions)

**Visual Elements:**
- Color-coded periodic table (metals, non-metals, metalloids)
- Falling element blocks with chemical symbols
- Group sorting zones
- Speed increases with level progression

### Lesson 3: The Dating Game (Ionic Bonding)

**Focus:** Ions, Cations, Anions, and Ionic Compounds

**Concept:** "The Give & Take" - Students act as "Matchmakers," pairing an electron-giver (Sodium) with an electron-taker (Chlorine) to create a stable couple

**Duration:** 20-25 minutes

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Define ions: atoms that gain or lose electrons to achieve stable electron configuration (full outer shell)
- Distinguish cations (positive ions - metals lose electrons) from anions (negative ions - non-metals gain electrons)
- Explain ionic bonding: electrostatic attraction between oppositely charged ions
- Write ionic formulas (e.g., Na‚Å∫ + Cl‚Åª ‚Üí NaCl, Mg¬≤‚Å∫ + 2Cl‚Åª ‚Üí MgCl‚ÇÇ)
- Describe properties of ionic compounds: high melting point, brittle, conduct when dissolved/molten

**Misconceptions to Address:**
- Ions and atoms are the same (ions have different charge and electron count)
- Metals and non-metals form covalent bonds (metal + non-metal = ionic)
- Ionic compounds conduct electricity as solids (only when dissolved/molten - ions must be mobile)

**Visual Elements:**
- Atom "profiles" showing electron shells
- Electron transfer animation (jumping from metal to non-metal)
- Matching interface with compatibility scores
- Crystal lattice formation visualization

### Lesson 4: Co-Parenting (Covalent Bonding)

**Focus:** Non-metal bonding & sharing electrons

**Interactive:** "Molecule Maker" - Connect atoms with "single," "double," or "triple" bond lines to satisfy the octet rule

**Duration:** 20-25 minutes

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Explain covalent bonding: non-metals share electrons to fill outer shells
- Distinguish single bonds (share 1 pair), double bonds (share 2 pairs), triple bonds (share 3 pairs)
- Apply octet rule: atoms want 8 electrons in outer shell (2 for hydrogen)
- Draw simple molecular structures (H‚ÇÇ, O‚ÇÇ, H‚ÇÇO, CO‚ÇÇ, CH‚ÇÑ)
- Describe covalent compound properties: low melting points, don't conduct electricity, often gases/liquids

**Misconceptions to Address:**
- Covalent bonds involve electron transfer (they share, not transfer)
- All bonds are the same strength (single < double < triple bond strength)
- Molecules and compounds are different (molecules are covalent compounds)

**Visual Elements:**
- Atom connection puzzle interface
- Bond-drawing tool (single/double/triple line options)
- Electron-counting helper
- Molecule stability indicator (green = octet satisfied, red = incomplete)
- 3D molecule viewer (optional AR feature)

### Lesson 5: The Sea of Electrons (Metallic Bonding)

**Focus:** Metal structure, conductivity, and malleability

**Simulation:** "Bend or Break" - Apply force to a metal lattice vs. an ionic lattice to see why one bends and the other shatters

**Duration:** 20-25 minutes

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Explain metallic bonding: positive metal ions in a "sea" of delocalized electrons
- Describe delocalized electrons: electrons not bound to specific atoms, free to move
- Relate structure to properties:
  - Conductivity (electrons move freely ‚Üí conduct electricity/heat)
  - Malleability (layers slide past each other without breaking bonds)
  - Luster (electrons interact with light)
  - High melting points (strong metallic bonds)
- Compare metallic vs ionic vs covalent structures

**Misconceptions to Address:**
- Metal atoms are bonded like molecules (metallic bonding is different - electron sea model)
- All solids have the same structure (ionic lattices vs metallic structures vs molecular crystals)
- Metals break like ionic compounds (metals bend/deform, ionic compounds shatter)

**Visual Elements:**
- Interactive lattice models (metal ions + electron clouds vs ionic crystal)
- Force application slider
- Deformation animation (metal layers sliding vs ionic bonds breaking)
- Side-by-side comparison view

- Mining just digs up pure metal (ore contains metal compounds mixed with rock - must be processed)
- All mining is underground (open-cut is very common for near-surface deposits)
- Mining sites are abandoned after use (modern regulations require rehabilitation)

**Visual Elements:**
- Strategy game interface (resource management)

## PHASE 1: THE PHYSICS OF POWER (ENERGY BASICS)

### Lesson 1: The Energy Shapeshifter

**Focus:** Transfers vs. Transformations (Kinetic, Potential, Heat, Light)

**Duration:** 20-25 minutes

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Identify energy forms: kinetic (movement), gravitational potential (height), elastic potential (stretched/compressed), thermal (heat), chemical (food, fuel), electrical, light, sound, nuclear
- Distinguish energy transfer (moving from one place to another) vs transformation (changing from one form to another)
- Trace energy transformations in systems:
  - Falling ball: GPE ‚Üí KE ‚Üí Sound + Heat (on impact)
  - Light bulb: Electrical ‚Üí Light + Heat
  - Photosynthesis: Light ‚Üí Chemical (stored in glucose)

**Misconceptions to Address:**
- Energy is created when you turn on a light (energy is transformed from electrical, not created)
- Potential energy is only gravitational (elastic, chemical potential also exist)
- Heat and temperature are the same (heat = energy transfer, temperature = average kinetic energy of particles)

**Visual Elements:**

### Lesson 2: The Law of Conservation

**Focus:** Energy is never created or destroyed

**Simulation:** "Skate Park Physics" - A skater on a ramp. Watch the Potential Energy bar drop as the Kinetic Energy bar rises. Total energy stays constant.

**Duration:** 20-25 minutes

**Learning Outcomes:**
- State Law of Conservation of Energy: Energy cannot be created or destroyed, only transformed or transferred
- Explain energy conservation in systems:
  - Pendulum: KE ‚Üî GPE (constant total, ignoring friction)
  - Skatepark: At top = max GPE, zero KE. At bottom = max KE, zero GPE. Total constant.
- Identify "lost" energy: not destroyed, transformed to less useful forms (usually heat + sound due to friction/air resistance)
- Understand perpetual motion is impossible (friction always converts useful energy to heat)

**Misconceptions to Address:**
- Energy "disappears" when things stop moving (it transforms to heat, doesn't disappear)
- Energy is lost to friction (it's not lost, it's transformed to heat - less useful but still energy)
- We can create energy from nothing (violates conservation law - impossible)

### Lesson 10: Heat Moves (Thermodynamics)

**Focus:** Conduction, Convection, Radiation

**Simulation:** "Thermal Vision" - View a house through a thermal camera. Spot the red (heat loss) zones at windows and gaps.

**Duration:** 20-25 minutes

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Define heat transfer: energy moving from hot objects to cold objects
- Explain three heat transfer methods:

  **CONDUCTION:**
  - Heat through direct contact (particle to particle)
  - Solids: particles vibrate, energy transfers to neighbors
  - Metals conduct well (free electrons carry heat)
  - Insulators conduct poorly (trapped air, no free electrons)
  - Example: Metal spoon gets hot in hot water, wooden spoon doesn't

  **CONVECTION:**
  - Heat through fluid (liquid/gas) movement
  - Hot fluid less dense ‚Üí rises. Cool fluid more dense ‚Üí sinks.
  - Creates circulation currents
  - Example: Hot air rises from heater, cool air sinks to floor (convection current)

  **RADIATION:**
  - Heat through electromagnetic waves (infrared)
  - Doesn't need particles (works in vacuum)
  - All objects emit radiation (hotter = more radiation)
  - Dark surfaces absorb/emit better than shiny surfaces
  - Example: Sun's heat reaches Earth through space (radiation)

- Apply to houses:
  - Conduction: heat through walls, windows (solid materials)
  - Convection: warm air escaping through gaps, cold drafts entering
  - Radiation: sun heating house through windows, heat radiating from walls at night
- Identify heat loss areas: windows (conduction + radiation), gaps/cracks (convection), thin walls/roofs (conduction)

**Misconceptions to Address:**
- Heat and cold flow (only heat flows - cold is absence of heat)
- Insulation creates heat (it slows heat transfer, doesn't generate heat)
- Radiation only comes from the sun (all objects radiate - amount depends on temperature)

**Visual Elements:**
- Thermal camera view of house (color-coded heat zones: red = hot, blue = cold)
- Click hotspots to see heat loss explanations
- Three animations side-by-side (conduction, convection, radiation with particle/fluid/wave visualization)
- Heat transfer rate comparison (metal vs wood, still air vs moving air, dark vs shiny surfaces)

### Lesson 11: Passive Solar Design

**Focus:** Orientation, Eaves, and Thermal Mass

**Interactive:** "Sun Tracker" - Rotate a virtual house to face North. Adjust the eave overhang to block summer sun but let in winter sun.

**Duration:** 20-25 minutes

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Explain passive solar design: using sun's energy for heating/lighting without mechanical systems
- Apply design principles in Southern Hemisphere (Australia):

  **ORIENTATION:**
  - Face main windows NORTH (maximum sun exposure)
  - North = sun path (sun travels east ‚Üí north ‚Üí west in Australia)
  - Minimize west windows (harsh afternoon sun in summer)

  **EAVES/SHADING:**
  - Eave overhang calculated for latitude
  - Summer: high sun angle (blocked by eaves ‚Üí shade)
  - Winter: low sun angle (enters under eaves ‚Üí warmth)
  - Rule of thumb: eave depth ‚âà 45¬∞ angle

  **THERMAL MASS:**
  - Heavy materials (concrete, brick, tiles) absorb heat during day, release at night
  - Stabilizes temperature (cooler in day, warmer at night)
  - Place in sunlight (north-facing) for maximum effect
  - Light materials (timber, plasterboard) don't store heat

  **VENTILATION:**
  - Cross-ventilation: windows on opposite sides allow breeze through
  - High windows: hot air escapes (stack effect)
  - Night purge: open windows at night to cool thermal mass

- Calculate eave depth for sun angle: depth = window height / tan(sun altitude angle)
- Recognize climate variations: tropical needs more shading, temperate balance, cold prioritizes winter sun capture

**Misconceptions to Address:**
- Passive solar works everywhere the same (climate/latitude variations critical)
- Big windows always mean more solar gain (north windows gain, west/east can overheat)
- Thermal mass is insulation (opposite - mass stores/releases heat, insulation prevents heat flow)

**Visual Elements:**
- Interactive house rotator (360¬∞ view, compass showing north)
- Sun path simulator (shows summer vs winter sun angle)
- Eave adjustment slider (see shadow change by season)
- Thermal mass demonstration (side-by-side: brick vs timber interior temperature graphs)
- Climate zone selector (tropical, temperate, cold - different optimal designs)

### Lesson 12: The Insulation Challenge

**Focus:** Materials (R-Value) and Double Glazing

**Project Prep:** This lesson directly feeds into the "Energy Efficient House" project (Weeks 9-10)

**Duration:** 20-25 minutes

**Learning Outcomes:**
- Define insulation: material that reduces heat transfer (slows conduction)
- Explain R-value: resistance to heat flow (higher = better insulation)
  - Units: m¬≤¬∑K/W (area √ó temperature difference / watts)
  - Common ranges: R1.5 (thin batts), R6.0 (thick ceiling batts), R10+ (extreme insulation)
- Compare insulation materials:

  | Material | R-value (per 100mm) | Pros | Cons |
  |----------|---------------------|------|------|
  | Air (still) | ~R2.5 | Free | Hard to trap (convection) |
  | Fiberglass batts | ~R2.5-3.0 | Cheap, common | Skin irritant, sags over time |
  | Polyester batts | ~R2.5 | Non-itchy, recyclable | Slightly more expensive |
  | Foam (polyurethane) | ~R4.0-5.0 | High R-value | Expensive, chemicals |
  | Reflective foil | ~R0.5-1.0 | Reflects radiation, cheap | Low R-value alone (use with air gap) |

- Understand insulation location priorities:
  1. Ceiling (hot air rises - highest heat loss in winter, gain in summer)
  2. Walls
  3. Floor (if above ground/crawl space)
- Explain double glazing: two glass panes with air/gas gap between
  - Single glass: ~R0.15 (poor insulator)
  - Double glazed: ~R0.3-0.4 (2x better)
  - Bonus: reduces condensation, noise
- Recognize energy star ratings for appliances: more stars = more efficient (saves running cost)

**Misconceptions to Address:**
- More insulation always better (diminishing returns - R6 to R7 adds little benefit for cost, R1 to R2 adds lots)
- Insulation keeps heat out AND in (yes! Slows heat flow in both directions - cool in summer, warm in winter)
- Reflective foil is sufficient alone (needs air gap to work - reflects radiation but doesn't resist conduction)

**Visual Elements:**
- Insulation material comparison table (R-values, cost, applications)
- Heat flow simulator (adjust insulation thickness, see temperature gradient)
- Double glazing cross-section diagram (showing air gap, heat flow reduction)
- Energy star rating decoder (what each star level means for running cost)
- "Build Your Wall" activity: stack insulation layers, calculate total R-value

**Project Connection:**

- Choose insulation R-values for ceiling/walls
- Position windows for passive solar gain
- Calculate thermal mass placement
- Select energy-efficient appliances
