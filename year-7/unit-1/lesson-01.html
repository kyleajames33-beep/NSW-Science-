üìö What is Science?

Definition of Science

Science is a systematic way of understanding the natural world through observation, experimentation, and evidence.

Science builds explanations that can be tested and revised based on new evidence.

Science vs Opinion

Science:

  • Based on evidence
  • Testable through experiments
  • Can be proven wrong
  • Revised with new data

Opinion:

  • Based on personal belief
  • Cannot be tested
  • Cannot be proven wrong
  • Personal preference

Evidence is Key üîë

Evidence = observations or measurements that support or challenge a claim

Without evidence, it's just a guess or opinion!

Evidence must be repeatable and verifiable by others.

What Science Studies

Science CAN study:

  • Natural phenomena (weather, rocks, stars)
  • Physical processes (motion, electricity)
  • Living things (cells, ecosystems)

Science CANNOT answer:

  • "What is beautiful?"
  • "What is right or wrong?"
  • "Why do we exist?"

What is Science?

Key Characteristics of Science:

  1. Based on evidence (not opinion or belief)
  2. Testable through experiments or observations
  3. Can be proven wrong (falsifiable)
  4. Revised when new evidence emerges
  5. Focuses on natural phenomena

Scientific claims must be supported by observations or measurements that can be repeated and verified by others.

üß™ Testing Claims Activity

For each claim below, think about:

  1. What evidence would be needed?
  2. How could it be tested?
  3. Could it be proven wrong?

Claim 1: "Tomato plants grow faster with fertilizer"

Evidence needed: Measure the growth of plants with and without fertilizer over several weeks.

How to test: Grow two groups of identical tomato plants - give one group fertilizer and the other none. Measure and compare growth.

Can it be proven wrong? Yes! If both groups grow at the same rate, the claim is false.

Claim 2: "Lucky charms help you win games"

Evidence needed: This cannot be tested scientifically!

Why? There's no scientific mechanism for how a charm could affect game outcomes. Wins and losses depend on skill, strategy, and chance - not objects.

This is NOT science - it's superstition or belief.

Claim 3: "Salt dissolves faster in hot water than cold water"

Evidence needed: Measure how long it takes salt to dissolve in water at different temperatures.

How to test: Put equal amounts of salt in equal amounts of hot and cold water. Time how long each takes to dissolve completely.

Can it be proven wrong? Yes! If cold water dissolves salt just as fast (or faster), the claim is false.

üéâ You've Discovered the Foundation of Science!

Evidence-based thinking is what separates science from opinion and belief.

Key Discovery

Science isn't just about wearing lab coats and mixing chemicals. It's a way of thinking that relies on evidence instead of guesses, opinions, or beliefs.

üí° The Nature of Scientific Knowledge

Why Evidence Matters

Science builds knowledge through systematic observation and testing. But not just any evidence will do - scientific evidence must meet specific criteria:

Empirical

Based on observation or measurement, not just ideas or thoughts.

Example: Measuring temperature with a thermometer (empirical) vs guessing "it feels hot" (not empirical)

Repeatable

Others must be able to observe the same thing if they follow the same procedure.

Example: If you drop a ball, it falls. Anyone else dropping a ball will see the same thing.

Objective

Independent of personal feelings or opinions.

Example: "The solution is purple" (objective) vs "The solution is pretty" (subjective opinion)

‚ö†Ô∏è Important Point

Without evidence, we're just guessing! Even if a guess turns out to be correct, it's not science until it's been tested with evidence.

How Science Differs from Other Ways of Knowing

Way of Knowing Based On Example
Science Evidence & testing "Water boils at 100¬∞C" (can measure and verify)
Opinion Personal preference "Vanilla is the best flavour" (subjective)
Belief Faith or tradition Religious or cultural beliefs (not testable)
Authority Trust in expert "My teacher said so" (could be verified, but not evidence itself)

üí° Key Insight

All of these ways of knowing are valid for different purposes! Science is the best tool for understanding the natural world, but it can't answer every type of question humans care about.

üìù Worked Examples

Let's practice identifying science vs opinion and understanding the role of evidence.

Example 1 (Easy): Identifying Science vs Opinion

Problem: Is this statement science or opinion? "Dogs are better pets than cats."

Step 1: Ask: "Can this be tested with evidence?"

‚Üí No - "better" is a personal judgment

Step 2: Ask: "Is this based on personal preference?"

‚Üí Yes - different people prefer different pets

Answer: Opinion - it's based on personal preference, not measurable evidence.

Example 2 (Medium): Evaluating Evidence

Problem: Someone claims "Eating carrots improves eyesight." Is this science? What evidence would you need?

Step 1: Identify what evidence would look like

‚Üí We would need to compare the eyesight of people who eat carrots vs those who don't

Step 2: Consider if it's testable

‚Üí Yes! We can measure vision before and after carrot consumption

Step 3: Could it be proven wrong?

‚Üí Yes - if studies show no difference in eyesight, the claim is false

Answer: This CAN be science if tested properly with evidence. (Note: Studies show carrots contain Vitamin A which is important for eye health, but eating extra carrots won't give you super-vision!)

Example 3 (Hard): Complex Scenario

Problem: A student says "I did better on my test because I wore my lucky socks." Is this scientific thinking? Why or why not?

Step 1: Identify the claim

‚Üí Lucky socks caused better test performance

Step 2: Is there a testable mechanism?

‚Üí No - there's no scientific mechanism for how socks could affect brain performance

Step 3: What other explanations exist?

‚Üí More study time, better sleep, easier test, improved confidence, coincidence

Step 4: Could this be tested fairly?

‚Üí Very difficult - you'd need to control all other variables (study time, sleep, test difficulty, etc.)

Answer: NOT scientific thinking - this confuses correlation (two things happening together) with causation (one thing causing another). Just because the socks and good grade happened together doesn't mean the socks caused the grade!

‚ùå Common Mistakes

Mistake 1: "If a scientist says it, it must be science"

‚Üí Wrong! Scientists can have opinions too. What makes something science is the evidence, not who said it.

Mistake 2: "Science proves things true forever"

‚Üí Wrong! Science is always open to revision with new evidence. That's a strength, not a weakness!

Mistake 3: "Experiments are the only form of scientific evidence"

‚Üí Wrong! Observations (like in astronomy or ecology) can also provide scientific evidence.

Mistake 4: "If something can't be tested, it's wrong"

‚Üí Wrong! It just means science can't answer that question. Many important questions (about art, ethics, meaning) are beyond science's scope.

üîç Mysteries Solved!

Remember those NSW mysteries from the start? Now you can solve them using scientific thinking:

üèñÔ∏è Bondi Beach Waves

Science can explain! Waves are caused by wind, tides, and ocean currents‚Äîall measurable phenomena. Scientists use weather data, tide tables, and ocean current models to predict wave sizes.

The dolphin claim? Not supported by evidence. Dolphins don't create large waves - they're too small compared to the ocean.

üèîÔ∏è Blue Mountains Haze

Scientists tested this! They collected air samples from the Blue Mountains and analyzed them chemically. They found eucalyptus oil droplets in the air, which scatter blue light.

This is evidence-based science: the claim was tested and confirmed with empirical data.

üêë Dubbo Farm Sheep

Scientific investigation could solve this! Scientists could test:

  • Soil quality near the creek vs other areas
  • Nutrient content in water and plants
  • Plant growth and variety near the creek

By collecting evidence, they could find the real reason (perhaps better grass, minerals in water, or shade from trees).
